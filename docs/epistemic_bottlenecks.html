epistemic bottlenecks

We know of some objects only through their effects on other objects to which we have comparatively direct epistemic access. Stars too faint to be seen by the naked eye, for example, are known to us only through their effects on photographic plates, the antennae of radio telescopes and other astronomical equipment. Let us call this kind of epistemic dependency an epistemic bottleneck; and speak of the objects, like faint stars, which are known only indirectly as being on the far side of the relevant bottleneck, and the objects through which they are known as being in the relevant bottleneck.

Many philosophers, most notably empiricists such as Berkeley and Hume, but also many non-empiricists including Aristotle, Descartes and Kant, have believed that there is a special type of object through which all others (or at least all other objects located in space and time) are known to us. If there are such sense-data they constitute a kind of ultimate epistemic bottleneck, on the far side of which were all the other objects in the world. The claim that there are epistemic bottlenecks, however, does not commit us to the existence of such an ultimate bottleneck nor the existence of sense data. Even if there is no ultimate epistemic bottleneck nor ultimate evidence upon which all our knowledge of a posteriori truths is based, and what is taken as evidence depends on context and passing interests, there may still be non-ultimate epistemic bottlenecks.

Although belief in sense-data is no longer as widespread as it was, it may be worthwhile to note several other properties that have traditionally been attributed to sense-data which are not required of the objects in epistemic bottlenecks as that term is being defined here:

need not be directly observed

The objects in one epistemic bottleneck, may themselves be on the far side of some other epistemic bottleneck, and known only by their effects on yet closer objects to which our epistemic access is again comparatively more direct.

In Russell's terminology the objects in an epistemic bottleneck need not be known by acquaintance but may be known by description. But the existence of epistemic bottlenecks, or rather of objects in epistemic bottlenecks, does not presume on Russell's theory of knowledge. Indeed the existence of epistemic bottlenecks does not imply that there are any epistemically closest objects that are known to us by acquaintance at all.

The claim that there are epistemic bottlenecks does not preclude there being proximal causes of experience without proximal objects of experience. Our awareness may always be of more or less distal objects*Least distal are those objects which directly impinge upon our skin or other sensory surfaces, and the firing of our sensory cells themselves. In psychological texts these are sometime described as proximal stimuli which are directly perceived by our sensory receptors. But of course such events are not directly perceived by us and are not known to us by acquaintance. By that rigorous standard such impingements still count as distal and there is a sense in which the firing of our sensory cells is not perceived by us at all except with the aid of special equipment (and generally in the third person). Let us avoid being drawn into just where to draw the line between proximal and distal stimuli for the time being.. If we perceive apples, chairs and people without there being intermediary sense-data which we perceive directly, that does not preclude the existence of epistemic bottlenecks.
